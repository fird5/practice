What You'll Learn

In Week 1 you learned to separate thinking modes. This week you go further — you will understand why predictability is the enemy of disruption and why AI, no matter how powerful, is fundamentally a pattern-completion machine.

When you ask AI "give me a creative idea," it searches its training data for the most statistically probable answer. That answer is, by definition, what everyone else has already thought of. Lateral Leaps is the antidote.

The Predictability Trap
🤖
What AI Does
Finds the highest-probability answer from billions of training examples. Safe, competent, forgettable.
🧠
What You Do
Force unexpected connections between unrelated concepts. Produces ideas that have never existed in training data.
📉
The Risk of Playing Safe
In a world where everyone uses AI, the "average" idea is free. Only unexpected ideas have commercial value.
🚀
The Lateral Leap
A deliberate mental jump to an unrelated stimulus — then forcing a connection back to your problem.

What You'll Learn

The Random Entry technique is De Bono's core Lateral Thinking tool. You pick a completely random word, object, or concept — with no logical connection to your problem — and then force yourself to build a bridge between them.

The randomness is the point. Your brain cannot predict what connections it will find, which means the output is genuinely novel. In this lesson you will learn the 5-step process and how to use Gemini as your stimulus generator.

The 5-Step Random Entry Process
1
Define the Problem Clearly State the challenge in one sentence. The more specific, the better. Vague problems produce vague leaps.
2
Generate a Random Stimulus Ask Gemini: "Give me one completely random noun — an object, animal, or concept." Accept the first response without filtering.
3
List Attributes of the Stimulus Ask Gemini to describe 5–8 properties of the random word. Don't think about your problem yet.
4
Force the Connection Take each attribute and ask: "How could this quality apply to my problem?" Resist the urge to discard uncomfortable connections.
5
Develop the Best Connections Pick the 2–3 most unexpected connections and ask Gemini to build them into full concepts. These are your lateral leaps.

What You'll Learn

Watch the full 5-step Random Entry process applied to a real SEA business problem in real time. You will see what a genuine lateral leap looks like — and importantly, what a fake leap looks like (when students just rename an obvious idea).

After this lesson you will run your own Random Entry session as warm-up for the Sprint, using a problem prompt provided in class.

Common Mistakes to Avoid
🚫
Filtering your random word. If Gemini gives you "jellyfish" and you ask for a "better" word, you've already killed the lateral leap. Randomness is non-negotiable.
🚫
Making a superficial connection. "My startup should be like a jellyfish because it's fluid" is not a lateral leap — it's a metaphor. Force deeper: what does a jellyfish's lack of a brain teach you about decentralised decision-making?
🚫
Stopping at one leap. Run the process 3–5 times with different random stimuli. Your best idea almost never comes from the first session.
🚫
Using obvious stimuli. Don't ask Gemini for a random word "related to business" or "related to technology." The stimulus must be genuinely unrelated to your problem domain.

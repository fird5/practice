What You'll Learn

Instagram Reels, TikTok, and LinkedIn video generate between three and five times the organic reach of static images on every major platform. The SEA creator economy is dominated by motion content — yet most students and SMEs still produce only static brand assets, because video production was expensive and technically demanding.

Luma Dream Machine changes this entirely. A single high-quality still image becomes a 5-second cinematic clip in under two minutes. Stitch three clips together and you have a 15-second social teaser that looks like it was shot with a professional cinematographer. This lesson teaches you to think in sequences, not frames.

Camera Movement Vocabulary
Dolly Push
Camera moves forward toward subject. Creates intimacy and reveals detail. Best for product reveals.
Crane Rise
Camera lifts vertically, revealing scale. Best for property and landscape establishing shots.
Orbital Pan
Camera circles subject at constant distance. Creates a premium, 360-degree product inspection feel.
Parallax Drift
Foreground and background move at different speeds. Creates cinematic depth on static scenes.
Slow Push In
Barely perceptible forward motion with slight zoom. The luxury standard — used by high-end property and fashion brands.
Ken Burns
Slow pan across a static image. Transforms any still photograph into a documentary-quality motion clip.

What You'll Learn

Luma Dream Machine takes an image plus a text prompt and generates a short cinematic clip. The text prompt controls camera movement, motion speed, lighting behaviour, and the overall cinematic feel of the generated video.

You will learn to structure a Luma motion prompt and — critically — how to maintain visual consistency across a sequence of clips that will be edited together. Inconsistent frame colour, lighting direction, or motion speed is the single biggest reason AI video looks cheap. You will learn to prevent all three.

Luma Motion Prompt Template — Real Estate
Use this structure for each clip in your sequence
[SHOT TYPE]: Slow push in, camera moves forward at imperceptible speed. [SUBJECT]: Luxury city-centre condominium living room. Floor-to-ceiling windows. KL skyline visible at golden hour. [MOTION]: Camera drifts 8% forward over 5 seconds. No shake. Silky smooth. [LIGHTING BEHAVIOUR]: Warm late-afternoon light shifts almost imperceptibly warmer across the duration. [FRAME RATE FEEL]: Cinematic 24fps — no video feel. Slightly desaturated in shadows, warm highlights. [MOOD]: "Open house for a buyer who has already decided." [CONSTRAINTS]: No people in frame. No motion blur. No obvious AI artifacts.

What to Avoid
🚫
Don't generate each clip from scratch. Use your best Recraft.ai image from Week 1 as the reference frame for your first Luma clip — this locks the visual identity across the entire sequence. Consistency starts at the source image.
🚫
Don't mix motion speeds between clips. A slow push cut to a fast orbital creates jarring discontinuity. Plan your shot sequence on paper first — each clip should have the same or slower motion than the previous one.
🚫
Don't neglect the constraints clause. "No AI artifacts" and "no motion blur" are not aesthetic preferences — they are technical requirements. Every Luma prompt needs these constraints, or you will spend more time regenerating than producing.

Sprint Tips from SitiRead before you begin
▼
🎯
Plan your 3-shot sequence before generating anything: Establishing shot → Feature shot → Close detail. This is the classic property video structure. Your Luma prompts should follow this arc, with each clip building intimacy.
🎯
Use the same Recraft.ai hero image as the source for your first Luma clip. This creates visual continuity between your static asset and your motion sequence — the mark of a professional multimodal production, not three disconnected experiments.
🎯
The highest-scoring submissions use a Suno AI track that matches the cinematic pace of the video — not just the brand. A slow-push condo walkthrough with a 128 BPM energetic track is a mismatch that judges will penalise. Make the audio direct the emotion the visuals set up.
